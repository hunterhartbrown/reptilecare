Leopard Gecko Care Guide

Leopard geckos, also referred to as leos, are small, colorful reptiles with a generally docile, slow-moving nature. They can be distinguished by the color and pattern of their tail, which features a combination of black stripes and spots on their backs and tails. A variety of morphs and mutations alter their coloration, leading to a wide range of prices at reptile retailers and expos. Typically, these reptiles cost between $100 and $250 USD, depending on the morph and age. A minimum 40-gallon enclosure is required, furnished with either paper towels or a well-mixed semiarid substrate. Leopard geckos are crepuscular/nocturnal, meaning they are usually inactive during the day and more active at dusk, although they may not always adhere to this sleep pattern. As insectivores, they need an enclosure that features both humid and dry areas. With proper care, leopard geckos can live between 15 and 20 years. Their ease of care, extensive captive-breeding efforts, and docile nature make them an ideal choice for beginners interested in reptile-keeping.

Species Info

Leopard Geckos, or Eublepharis macularius, are a species of gecko found in the deserts of the Middle East and southwestern Asia. More specifically, their native habitat range includes the rocky, dry grasslands and desert regions of Afghanistan, Pakistan, India, Nepal, and parts of Iran. As previously mentioned, they are known for their distinctive spots and gentle nature. In the wild, leos gather most of their heat from rocks warmed by the sun throughout the day. They are smaller than many other reptiles, with hatchlings typically measuring anywhere from 3 to 4 inches long. Adults reach full size at around 12–18 months, with females growing to 7–8 inches and males to 8–11 inches. Their toes do not contain any adhesive like that found in tree-dwelling geckos, so they cannot climb smooth walls. Leopard geckos have distinctly thick tails that store fat and extra energy, similar to camel humps. This is part of what makes them such a great beginner-friendly reptile, as they can typically go several days in a row without meals. When hunting, leos sometimes lift their tails in a wagging motion as they approach their prey. Leos can also make faint chirping noises when they are curious, excited, or nervous. Unlike most other geckos, leos have eyelids.

Habitat Size Requirements

Leopard Geckos need a habitat that is at least 10 gallons in size for a single gecko. More space is needed if housing multiple geckos.

Heating Requirements

Leopard Geckos require a temperature gradient in their enclosure, with a warm side between 88-92°F (31-33°C) and a cool side around 70-75°F (21-24°C).

Humidity / Airflow Requirements

Leopard Geckos thrive in low humidity environments, typically around 30-40%. Ensure adequate ventilation to prevent respiratory issues.

Food Requirements

Leopard Geckos primarily eat insects such as crickets, mealworms, and dubia roaches. Dust their food with calcium and vitamin D3 supplements.

Opportunities for Enrichment

Provide hiding spots, climbing structures, and varying substrates to keep your Leopard Gecko engaged and comfortable.
