Eastern Collared Lizard Care Guide

Eastern Collared Lizards (Crotaphytus collaris) are diurnal, medium-sized lizards native to the south-central United States and northern Mexico, renowned for their bright colors and black “collars.” Adults reach 8–14 inches (20–35 cm) total length, with powerful heads and jaws used for subduing prey and in territorial displays (ReptiFiles).

In the wild they occupy rocky outcrops, desert scrub, and grasslands, often basking on stones in full sun. Capable of bipedal sprinting up to 16 mph (24 km/h) when threatened, they are solitary and territorial; males may fight or cannibalize conspecifics if housed together. With proper husbandry they can live over 10 years in captivity (Britannica).

Provide a minimum terrarium of 36″ L × 18″ W × 18″ H (90 × 45 × 45 cm) for one adult, though larger (e.g., 48″ × 24″ × 24″) is highly recommended to allow natural movement. Use a front-opening glass or PVC enclosure with a secure mesh top for ventilation and escape prevention (ReptiFiles).

Line the floor with 4 inches (10 cm) of a 60/40 topsoil–play-sand mix to simulate natural burrowing substrate. Spot-clean daily, fully replace every 3–4 months, and switch to paper towels if your lizard is ill or has skin issues. Include at least two hides (one on the warm side, one on the cool side) and a large, flat basking rock for thermoregulation.

Establish a thermal gradient with a basking spot of 105–115 °F (40–46 °C) and a cooler retreat of 75–85 °F (24–29 °C). Nighttime lows of 68–75 °F (20–24 °C) are acceptable. Use a 50–75 W halogen or incandescent flood lamp positioned 8–12″ above a flat rock for belly-warming basking (ReptiFiles).

For UVB, install a T5 HO 10.0 UVB tube running at least half the cage length, mounted 6–8″ above the basking surface to achieve a UV index of ~4–6. Supplement with a daylight white lamp (6500 K) to fully illuminate the enclosure (Arcadia). All lamps on a 12 h on/12 h off timer.

Maintain ambient humidity at 30–50% using a digital hygrometer to monitor. Ensure strong airflow with a screen top or side vents to prevent respiratory issues (ReptiFiles).

Provide a humid hide (~70% humidity) on the cool side using damp sphagnum moss or paper towels. Mist lightly and allow enclosure to dry within hours to avoid mold or bacterial growth. Always remove uneaten moisture sources daily.

Feed a varied diet of gut-loaded insects (crickets, Dubia roaches, locusts, superworms, BSFL) daily for juveniles; adults 3–4 times per week. Offer prey no larger than the lizard’s head and release them into the enclosure for hunting behavior (ReptiFiles).

Dust feeders with calcium powder (with D₃) at every feeding and a reptile multivitamin once weekly. Occasionally (monthly) offer adult feeders (pinkie mice or small anoles) as treats for adults (MO Conservation).

Provide fresh, dechlorinated water daily in a shallow dish and mist decor in the morning to encourage drinking from droplets.

Include multiple climbing structures (rock piles, sturdy branches) firmly anchored for perching and basking. Provide secure hides on both warm and cool sides for security (ReptiFiles).

Offer feeding enrichment by scattering live prey and using feeding tongs for hand-feeding. Add leaf litter, faux or live arid plants, and textured surfaces for digging and exploration. Supervised, secure out-of-enclosure time can further stimulate natural behaviors.
